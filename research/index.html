Are Uniform or Fragmented Carbon Taxes Optimal? Evidence from Canadian Manufacturing (Draft coming soon!)

A uniform carbon tax applied to all emission sources is optimal when there are no other distortions. Yet, observed carbon prices are highly fragmented both across and within countries, and multiple market imperfections exist. Should jurisdictions work towards a global carbon price when markets are imperfect? Using two confidential manufacturing plant-level datasets that span 18 years for Canada, I document large and persistent differences in the relative carbon tax-inclusive price of electricity to fossil fuels across space and time. Then, I build and estimate a quantitative model where firms choose their energy mix, which includes a fossil fuel aggregate and electricity. Firms are heterogeneous in their productivity and fossil fuel intensity, and regions have varying degrees of clean power. I apply the model to the Canadian manufacturing sector to estimate the impact of carbon taxes on emissions and output. I find that uniform carbon prices do not equalize the marginal abatement costs across regions, implying that the optimal tax is not uniform. However, while optimal carbon taxes are highly fragmented, the costs of carbon tax harmonization are small, with a 0.05 percentage point difference in output across both regimes.
Presented at CEA 2024
